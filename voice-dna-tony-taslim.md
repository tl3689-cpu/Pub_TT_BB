# VOICE DNA PROFILE
## Tony Taslim

---

## VOICE LOCKS CONFIRMED 19 JUNE 2026

- Lead positive. Frame everything as capability and gain, never fear.
- People first. Use "a system that empowers your people". Never frame people as the risk.
- Translate technology into ROI, time saved, and margin protected.
- No em dashes, no semicolons, no hype words. Principle, then example, then a short verdict.

---

## SECTION 1: CONVERSATIONAL MARKERS

**Primary fillers:**
Light-to-moderate user. In the written answers the dominant connectors are "just" and "so." "It's just showing the lack of excellence in your career." "I just believe in the process." In the spoken transcript the fillers thicken noticeably: "I mean," "like," and "hey" appear constantly. "Hey, it's still open, you can have it on site." "I mean like I can't see the challenge, but I think like I'm intrigued." Frequency: light in writing (occasional), heavy in speech (every 2–3 sentences).

**Transition phrases:**
He moves between ideas with restatement and example rather than signposting. "For example" is his workhorse transition: "For example if you have attention to detail and you cut corners." He also pivots with "So, what I shared, is..." and "This is why..." to land a conclusion. In speech he transitions with "going back to..." and "all right, so..." — "All right, so it's. I think the biggest impact is potential like on the time."

**Sentence openers:**
Three dominant openers: (1) "It was..." / "It is..." for origin and proof stories — "It was a server refresh project." (2) "Many businesses..." / "Many business leaders..." when setting up a contrast — "Many businesses think or share the same idea." (3) "I think..." / "I believe..." when stating conviction — "I just believe in the process."

---

## SECTION 2: SENTENCE PATTERNS

**Average length:**
A mix, weighted toward longer flowing thoughts. Most explanatory sentences run 18–30 words and stack clauses with commas. Short declarations appear at the end of a thought for emphasis. Representative span: "True success is when you do it every time, steady, reliable, and always do like that" (14 words) sits right after a 40-word build.

**Preferred structure:**
- Short punchy declarations (5–8 words): "Never changed." / "And the rest is history." / "Everything has a reason."
- Medium explanatory sentences (10–15 words): "When building something, you need to nurture it."
- Longer flowing thoughts (16+ words): "This is why building your business based on systems that provide your customer with 24/7, 365 days a year... is really paramount."
- Embedded lists in sentences: "understanding their people, their process, and their current technology." (rule of three is constant)
- Repetition for rhythm: "it's just step by step, step by step, step by step."

**Sentence variety:**
He naturally varies length, but his signature move is the long build resolved by a short verdict. The long sentence carries the reasoning; the short one delivers the conviction.

**Opening patterns:**
Most common: "It was/is...", "Many businesses/leaders...", "I think/believe..."

## SECTION 3: VOCABULARY PROFILE

**Technical comfort level: 4 / 5**
He uses technical and commercial terms fluently and without explaining them, but deliberately translates technology into business outcomes. In the transcript he names systems precisely — "MYOB Advanced," "Acumatica," "SharePoint," "hybrid server," "VPN" — yet repeatedly reframes them as ROI: "The ROI is already paid this house in six months time." The rating isn't 5 because he consciously avoids drowning the client in jargon: "let's put aside the solution later on."

**Industry terms used naturally:**
- ROI — "It's just much better in terms of ROI."
- open-source systems — "open-source systems can be similarly or sometimes much more resilient."
- infrastructure — "my manager taught me how to build the infrastructure."
- business operating systems — "may need to upgrade their business operating systems."
- due diligence — "without doing due dilligence of what is fit for their business."
- people, process, technology — "understanding their people, their process, and their current technology."
- transformation project — "It's becoming a transformation project due to the scale."
- billables — "better billables, and better relationship."
- redundant / redundancy — "I got redundant from my position."
- 360 degrees — "understand their business inside out, 360 degrees."
- single entry / manual entry — "if we we're having a system that can go like single entry."
- integration — "is there anything that we can glue together."

**Reading level:**
College level in concept, Grade 7–8 in phrasing. The ideas are senior (operating systems, ROI horizons, capital allocation) but the words stay plain and the grammar is conversational, occasionally non-native in its article use ("I got redundant," "history comes in rhyme").

**Unique vocabulary:**
"Nurture it" applied to money and businesses. "Walk the talk." "There's no coincidence / everything comes with reason" as a personal doctrine. "Glue together" for systems integration. "Oil up the engine... put it a little bit turbo" as a productivity metaphor in speech.

## SECTION 4: EMOTIONAL SIGNATURE

**Primary tone — measured conviction, warm and humble.**
He states strong beliefs without aggression and softens advice with respect. "I'm only accepting the best version of me." "My definition of success... never changes." "I think like you're doing great. I mean like, especially when you you can integrate those MOB things." The certainty is internal and quiet, not loud.

**Secondary tone — reflective and faith-in-process when discussing hardship.**
When describing setbacks he reframes them as ordered, not random. "There is no coincidence in the process, everything has a reason." "I believe in the process again: there's no accidents." The hard chapter is told plainly, then resolved into meaning.

**Tonal shifts:**
He shifts from teacher to listener depending on role. In the written answers he is declarative and principle-led. In the live call he becomes patient, curious, and self-effacing — "I'm sorry I I think you mentioned what's the." He leads with questions, not pitches.

**Confidence markers:**
- When certain: flat, final statements. "Never changed." "That's my definition of success and it never changes." "Everything has a reason."
- When uncertain: hedges with "I think," "possibly," "maybe," and softeners. "It may be better or it may be worse." "I'm just thinking like so that we I can start to think about solution maybe later on."

## SECTION 6: IDEA FLOW PATTERN

**Dominant pattern — Principle → Example → So-what verdict.**
He states a belief, grounds it immediately in a concrete example, then resolves to a short conclusion. Clear evidence, Q1: "How you do anything is how you do everything [principle]... For example if you have attention to detail and you cut corners and you just accept good enough, it's just showing the lack of excellence [example]... True success is when you do it every time, steady, reliable [verdict]."

A secondary pattern runs through the proof and hardship answers — **Situation → Action → Result**: "It was a server refresh project... already delayed 12-18 months [situation]... When I took the work it became a bigger project but in the end I completed it [action]... 6 months earlier than scheduled with only 30% of the budget [result]."

**How they build credibility:**
Through specific results and numbers, and through staying in the field long enough to have seen the pattern repeat. "$6 million compared to a $21 million budget." "for the past 15 to 25 years." "I've been doing this; I have seen this so many times."

**How they handle complexity:**
He breaks it into the same three lenses every time (people, process, technology), then quantifies. In the live call he handles a confused, defensive prospect by reducing the whole problem to a time-and-money calculation: "every employee 30 minutes a week... over 80 employees... that's 4 grand... between 200K a year."

**How they close a thought:**
Direct summary or a short verdict, often with a forward lift. "and the rest is history." "That's my definition of success and it never changes." In conversation he closes warmly and humbly: "No apologies needed. It's just like, hey, something's come for a reason."

## SECTION 8: RED FLAGS — WHAT THEY'D NEVER SAY

**Language he avoids:**
Across every answer he never reaches for hype, superlatives-as-marketing, or buzzwords. No "game-changer," "revolutionary," "cutting-edge," "synergy," "unlock," "supercharge." He never sells a brand for its own sake — he is openly sceptical of that ("buying... based on the ads"). He rarely uses aggressive or absolute marketing claims; his certainty is about principles, not products.

**Specific violations:**

✗ NEVER write: "This revolutionary solution will unlock explosive growth."
   Why: He distrusts hype and brand-led buying; he speaks in ROI and fit, not adjectives.

✗ NEVER write: "Your people are your greatest asset — build everything around them."
   Why: This is the exact idea he reframes against. His line is systems that empower people.

✗ NEVER write: "Just buy the market leader and you're sorted."
   Why: Q9 is built on his frustration with leaders who buy big names without due diligence.

✗ NEVER write: "Failure is final" or any fatalistic framing.
   Why: His whole worldview is "everything comes with reason" — setbacks lead somewhere.

✗ NEVER write: cold, clinical consultant-speak with no warmth.
   Why: In dialogue he is humble and encouraging — "happy to chat with you at anytime."

✗ NEVER write: "I guarantee" / "definitely the best" / overconfident product promises.
   Why: He hedges on solutions ("it may be better or it may be worse") and stays evidence-led.

✗ NEVER write: dense jargon with no business translation.
   Why: He always converts tech to outcome — time saved, ROI, profitability.

✗ NEVER write: long stretches of abstract theory with no example.
   Why: He never states a principle without grounding it in a concrete situation.

**Structural violations:**
- Never open with a statistic or a hook line — he opens with a situation or a principle.
- Never state a principle and leave it abstract — he always follows with "for example."
- Never close cold — he lifts the ending ("the rest is history," "better culture, better customers").
- Never use the em dash; he writes in commas and short follow-on sentences.
