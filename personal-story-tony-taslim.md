# PERSONAL STORY DOCUMENT
## Tony Taslim

---

## SECTION 1: ORIGIN & FOUNDATION

### The Moment That Changed Everything
It was the time I learned from T. Harv Eker: "How you do anything is how you do everything." That showed me a little bit of my habit, my focus, and my integrity in day-to-day tasks is reflected in my overall character and how I do things in daily life.

For example, if you have attention to detail and you cut corners and you just accept good enough, it's showing the lack of excellence in your career or relationship or in your finance.

It also brings the other point, which is the subconscious habits, how you handle small things. Is it your messy workplace? How punctual are you coming to work? Just small things. On personal finance it's how you manage complex or high-stakes situations, and consistency. True success is when you do it every time. Steady, reliable, and always do it like that.

### The Person Who Shaped Them
The biggest person still stuck in my mind is Harv Eker: "How you do anything is how you do everything." It sticks because it brings me into attention to detail. I'm only accepting the best version of me, whether my thought, my process, or my progress. Everything needs to be excellent.

Subconsciously, my habit is that I'm always trying to be punctual and on time when I'm saying something. I always walk the talk. I always do whatever I said when I promise anything, and I'm always consistent. I'm not changing here and there. When I'm saying I will deliver this, I deliver it. I never change how I do things when I'm saying that's what is always right. Never changed. I change only when the fact says so.

### The Value Running Their Decisions
When building something, you need to nurture it. For example, when you are accumulating wealth, build a great relationship with money. Only spend money to invest. Respect money, treat money well, and it will treat you well.

---

## SECTION 2: CHARACTER UNDER PRESSURE

### The Hardest Chapter
When I lost my job for the very first time, I got made redundant from my position. That really hit hard, and I couldn't find another job for another 3–4 months after that. It was even harder because I had invested all my savings in a car so that I could do my work, and after 3 months I got made redundant.

I needed to persevere, be resilient in finding work, keep communicating with people, agents, friends, and anyone. And the good thing is, after 4 months I got a great job that actually required a vehicle to get to the workplace. There is no coincidence in the process. Everything has a reason.

### The Risk That Defined Them
I don't have any specific decision that genuinely scared me, but the moment someone makes the decision to get married is really hard. I didn't know what becoming a husband was like, and after that I didn't know what the decision was like when I became a father for the first time. That's really hard.

But when you've already made that decision, you just do it. It's step by step, step by step, step by step, and you keep communicating with the people around you. If there's an issue, that's what helps me get through becoming married, becoming a husband, and becoming a dad for the first time.

### The Mistake That Built Them
It was my decision to blow all my savings to buy a car so that I could get a new job as an IT systems engineer, and it only lasted three months before I got made redundant. After three or four months of being redundant, I landed a better, much more lucrative role with much more experience, much more learning, much more growth in my career. That brought me to the next level. I just believe in the process: there's no coincidence, everything comes with a reason.

The same thing happened after that. I moved on to a new position, a new industry with new people. I believed in the process again. There were no accidents, because that position elevated me further up the career ladder. I became more senior, I learned more, and I became better within my career, with my network and many other things.

### Standing Their Ground
It was my decision to stop my working career and my last position at IBM, which I believe was the top of my career ladder, as an IT architect for IBM. I dealt with the latest and greatest system software possible, and I moved on to build my current business, which was a new thing. I started to learn how to do marketing and how to change my mindset. It grew me financially, emotionally, and relationship-wise, with new people. Initially I invested in myself and some business setup with quite a bit of money, around $100,000, but I think I recouped those investments, and the rest is history.

---

## SECTION 3: THE WORK & THE WHY

### When They Knew
It was my first job. On a day-to-day basis the first three months were just helping people with Microsoft Office, setting up emails and the website, and many simple day-to-day things. After a while my manager taught me how to build the infrastructure, whether Microsoft-based or open-source-based.

When I learned about open-source systems it blew my mind. I was like, wow, open-source systems can be similarly resilient, or sometimes much more resilient, than the paid Microsoft one. It just clicked. I'm fascinated that it worked, people paid for it, and people were happy with it. It's much better in terms of ROI.

### What They Do Differently
Care has many meanings for me. Caring for customers is paramount: understanding their people, their process, and their current technology. Understanding those three aspects so that I can understand their business inside out, 360 degrees.

Then I try to understand their goals, where they are at, and then make a strategy for how to get from where they are right now to where they want to be, what they feel good about. Then I help them implement those systems to reach the goal. This can mean using open-source systems, a subscription model, or even commercial systems, if I can see they fit their business, their people, their processes, and bring a better ROI.

### The Reframe That Changes Everything
Many businesses share the same idea, which is to build a business based on your people. But this digital age requires a system. So what I share is to build your business based on a system that empowers your people.

In this digital age the business often requires a 24/7 working schedule. Sometimes your people can only work a third of the time every day, which is 8 hours per day. Sometimes you also miss the weekends, 24 to 48 hours. That's the major gap in any business that requires 24/7 availability.

This is why building your business based on systems that provide your customer with 24/7, 365 days a year, which also supports and empowers your people to provide a better service, is really paramount and should be paramount for your business.

### The Work That Made Them Proud
It was a server refresh project, initially already delayed for 12–18 months. It became a transformation project due to the scale, the budgeting, and the criticality of the systems.

When I started, it had already been delayed 12–18 months due to the commercial process. When I took the work it became a bigger project, but in the end I completed it. The project finished 6 months earlier than scheduled, with only 30% of the budget, roughly $6 million compared to a $21 million budget. The total delivery time was compressed by half. We were meant to deliver it in 18 months, but we delivered in 9–10 months. That's an actual saving in delivery time, a saving in budget, and stakeholders were happy.

## SECTION 4: WORLDVIEW & DIRECTION

### What Everyone Gets Wrong
Many business leaders buy a branded commercial system or software based on the ads, or because it has good marketing. For example, big names like Microsoft, Google, or similar brand names.

What frustrates me is that they are not fully utilising what they buy, what they subscribe to, and it's not good for their ROI. I've been doing this; I have seen it so many times. I think it keeps happening because people want comfort. When they get money, they just want to spend it without doing due diligence on what fits their business, their people, their processes. They buy it just because it's easy to get.

### How Success Changed Shape
My mission hasn't changed for the past 15 to 25 years: helping as many people, as many businesses, as many organisations, even the country, as I can, through productivity using technology. When I can help as many of those as possible and earn a living from it, that is my success. Of course I also need to support my family, my people, my neighbours, everyone around me, so they can be successful as well. That's my definition of success, and it never changes.

### Where This Is All Going
I think my industry, the IT industry, will be similar to what happened in 2000, the dot-com bubble. At the moment it could be an AI bubble, but it may be better or it may be worse. History rhymes. What I think is that now AI will transform many businesses, similar to how the internet and websites did in the early 2000s.

Yes, there will be people who need more training on using AI, and there will be a transformation of businesses. Traditional businesses may need to upgrade their business operating systems so they can serve their customers better, serve their people better, and have better processes. In the end, when they can do that and package it up, it becomes a better, more productive business, with better profitability, better culture, and better customers they serve.

### What People Actually Get
They will have more clarity in their business. They get more purpose about what they are doing, and they feel they can be more efficient and more productive by doing what they like, what they love, and what they should be doing, instead of manual work and administration.

On top of that, they feel more confident because they have support. They have the best support, so they can focus on what they are doing with the support of the systems. More efficient in the process, more effective in the result. They attract better clients, better-paying clients, better billables, and better relationships with family, relatives, friends, colleagues, and better networks.
